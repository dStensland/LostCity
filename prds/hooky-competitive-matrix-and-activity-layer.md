## Purpose

This document answers two questions:

1. Where does Hooky currently sit versus the practical competitor set?
2. What important family-planning whitespace is still under-owned, especially beyond camps and structured programs?

It also adds the missing strategic layer:

- **activities / places to go**

That layer is essential if Hooky wants to solve the real family coordination problem instead of becoming only a camp-and-program portal.

---

## Executive Read

Hooky is now strong enough to compete on **planning quality**, but not yet on total **breadth perception**.

The key strategic truth is:

- competitors still feel broader
- Hooky is increasingly more useful where it has coverage

That is a good place to be, because breadth can be expanded from a strong data foundation. Trust and structure are harder to add later.

The next major opportunity is not just "more programs." It is building a true **family activity layer** that helps solve:

- "What can we do today?"
- "What works for this age band?"
- "What fills the gap between other plans?"
- "What's worth doing when we don't need a formal program?"

That is where Hooky can become more like a family operating system and less like a better directory.

## Competitive Position Matrix

Competitor reference set:

- `Atlanta Parent`
- `Mommy Poppins`
- `Kids Out and About`
- `ActivityHero`

### High-level read

| Area | Hooky vs market | Read |
|---|---|---|
| structured program data | **ahead** | official-source, compareable, better normalized than competitor directories |
| school camps | **near parity** | now one of Hooky's strongest lanes |
| museum / institution camps | **ahead** | strong trust and field quality |
| STEM / specialty | **near parity** | strong core, but still concentrated in a few operators |
| arts / performance | **near parity** | premium core is good; long tail is still thinner |
| civic / municipal | **behind, improving fast** | now materially live, but still less mature and less hardened |
| swim / movement | **behind** | useful, but still too narrow versus market breadth |
| editorial packaging / roundup feel | **behind** | competitors still feel fuller at a glance |
| compare / decision support | **ahead in strategy, partial in execution** | the product idea is stronger than any competitor, but surface quality still needs to catch up |
| school-calendar-triggered utility | **ahead in strategy, mostly unclaimed in market** | very few competitors own this meaningfully |

### What competitors actually beat Hooky at

Competitors mostly win on:

- provider breadth
- category completeness at the glance layer
- editorial discovery packaging
- long-tail neighborhood provider sprawl

They do **not** generally win on:

- trustable structure
- age/date/price normalization
- official registration intelligence
- compareable program objects
- planning workflow integration

## Competitor-by-Competitor Read

### `Atlanta Parent`

**Wins on:**

- broad summer-camp and provider discovery
- "everything is here somewhere" perception
- category completeness

**Weakness:**

- mostly a discovery and roundup layer
- lower structural advantage
- weaker compare and planning utility

**Hooky response:**

- match enough breadth to avoid feeling thin
- beat it decisively on decision support

### `Mommy Poppins`

**Wins on:**

- intent-led packaging
- polished editorial framing
- family-use-case collections

**Weakness:**

- less systematic program structure
- still not a strong compare or coordination product

**Hooky response:**

- steal the best packaging logic, not the editorial model
- build stronger "planning moment" surfaces instead of listicles

### `Kids Out and About`

**Wins on:**

- long-tail breadth
- local provider sprawl
- category coverage

**Weakness:**

- lower signal quality
- thinner trust and curation

**Hooky response:**

- use it as a discovery map, not a product benchmark
- Hooky should never look that noisy

### `ActivityHero`

**Wins on:**

- more program-structured discovery than editorial competitors
- stronger family-activity directory shape

**Weakness:**

- still less local and trust-rich than a strong official-source graph
- less integrated with local school/calendar pressure

**Hooky response:**

- this is the closest strategic comparison
- Hooky should aim to beat it by being more local, more calendar-aware, and more trustworthy

## Where Hooky Is Strongest Right Now

These are the lanes where Hooky is already building a serious moat:

1. **School camps**
   Hooky now has enough strong official school sources that this lane feels real, not speculative.

2. **Institution-led family programming**
   Museums, aquarium, zoo, botanical, and similar sources are one of Hooky's best trust layers.

3. **Structured program intelligence**
   Even where breadth is incomplete, Hooky is much closer than competitors to real comparison support.

4. **Civic/public upside**
   This was a weakness; it is now a promising lane. The grouped sweep proves it can matter at scale.

---

## Where Hooky Is Still Clearly Weak

1. **Long-tail sports and movement**
   Swim is started, but the broader movement universe is still under-mapped.

2. **Activities for "do something today" moments**
   Hooky has events and programs, but not yet a strong destination/activity layer for spontaneous family decisions.

3. **Neighborhood utility outside structured programs**
   Families often need a place, not an event.

4. **Perceived breadth at first glance**
   Even when Hooky has better data, competitors can still look fuller to a casual parent.

## The Big Whitespace Nobody Really Owns

This is the real strategic upside.

### 1. No-school-day planning intelligence

Parents do not want another generic roundup.

They want:

- what is actually available
- by age
- near me
- during the right hours
- with enough confidence to act quickly

This is still weakly served across the market.

### 2. Decision-grade compare for camps and programs

Many products help families **find** options.

Very few help families **choose** among them cleanly.

This remains one of Hooky's clearest product advantages.

### 3. Gap-filling coordination

This is the most under-owned job:

- Kid A has one commitment
- Kid B needs something nearby
- parent has a limited time window

This is closer to logistics software than content, and that is exactly why it is valuable.

### 4. Age-honest family planning

The market overuses "family-friendly."

Whitespace exists in:

- clearly toddler-safe
- actually good for elementary kids
- worth it for tweens
- acceptable for multi-age siblings

### 5. Public + premium blended planning

Most competitors separate:

- camp guides
- event calendars
- free things to do
- classes

Hooky can blend them into one planner:

- free civic option
- premium camp
- museum destination
- weather-safe backup

### 6. School-calendar-triggered action

This is still one of the least-owned but highest-leverage spaces in the category.

Competitors may mention school breaks. They do not generally turn them into an operating layer.

## The Missing Layer: Activities / Places To Go

Hooky should explicitly add a third major family-inventory layer:

1. `events`
2. `programs`
3. `activities`

### What the activity layer is

Activities are **destination-grade things families can do** even when there is no specific dated event or enrollable program.

Examples:

- arcades
- escape rooms
- ropes courses
- tubing / river outfitters
- petting zoos and animal encounter farms
- museums and childrenâ€™s museums
- science centers
- indoor play spaces
- trampoline parks
- mini golf
- outdoor adventure parks
- seasonal family attractions
- easy family hikes / boardwalk nature sites

This is not event data and not program data.

It is a structured family-place layer.

### Why it matters

Because real family planning includes:

- "we need something fun today"
- "we need a weather backup"
- "we need an all-ages option"
- "we have two hours to kill"
- "we have visitors in town"
- "camp is out, what now?"

Competitors only partially solve this, usually through weak listicles or generic attraction pages.

### Why Hooky should care now

Because the activity layer makes the rest of the portal better:

- fills weekend and no-school gaps when event inventory is thin
- broadens utility beyond enrollment-heavy moments
- gives Hooky more perennial value
- supports weather, age-band, and gap-filling use cases
- creates a stronger "always useful" surface than camps alone

## Activity-Layer Strategy

### Canonical object

An `activity` should be a durable family-place record with planning metadata.

Core fields:

- activity name
- operator / venue
- activity type
- neighborhood / area
- indoor / outdoor / mixed
- age suitability by band
- best-for tags
- duration expectation
- typical cost band
- reservation required / recommended / walk-up
- weather sensitivity
- stroller / accessibility notes when known
- food / restrooms / parking confidence when known
- destination URL

### Suggested activity taxonomy

Use broad parent-intent buckets, not tourism taxonomy:

1. indoor play
2. animals / nature
3. museums / learning
4. adventure / climbing / ropes
5. games / arcades / mini golf
6. hands-on / maker / creative
7. water / splash / tubing
8. low-lift neighborhood outings

### Age-band framing

This layer should be especially strong on age honesty:

- toddler
- preschool
- elementary
- tween
- teen
- multi-age sibling friendly

### Product surfaces this powers

- `What can we do today?`
- `Rainy day with kids`
- `Good for tweens`
- `Visitors this weekend`
- `2-hour gap fill`
- `Low-effort family outing`
- `Worth driving for`

## Why LostCity Has An Unfair Advantage Here

This is where cross-portal intelligence matters.

Hooky does not need to build the family activity layer from scratch in a vacuum.

LostCity already has advantages from the broader network:

1. **Venue graph leverage**
   Museums, attractions, outdoor places, and experience venues already exist or can be enriched once for many surfaces.

2. **Cross-portal demand signals**
   Other portals help reveal which destinations matter repeatedly, not just seasonally.

3. **Shared place intelligence**
   Hours, amenities, neighborhood relevance, indoor/outdoor character, and "worth going" signals can benefit multiple products.

4. **Consumer/B2B compounding**
   A family-friendly museum is useful to Hooky, hotels, concierge surfaces, city portals, and AI answers.

5. **Permanent-place infrastructure**
   LostCity already had to learn what a permanent attraction is versus an event. That work can now be turned from cleanup logic into product value.

This matters strategically because Hooky is not just another parenting site. It is sitting on top of a broader local-discovery infrastructure layer.
