## Purpose

This document answers a specific strategic question:

If Hooky wants to match or exceed competitors on **program depth**, what provider universe do those competitors imply, and which official sources should we target before building more individual crawlers?

This is a discovery map, not a sourcing shortcut.

**Rule:** Competitors are used for provider discovery and category discovery only. They are **not** the source of truth for Hooky events or programs. Every target provider must still be audited and sourced from official sites, public registration platforms, or official calendars.

## What Competitors Are Actually Covering

The strongest insight from the audit is that competitors are not winning on structure. They are winning on **provider breadth** and **category completeness**.

Atlanta Parent’s camp guide is the clearest signal. It organizes the market into these buckets:

1. Academic and specialty camps
2. Art camps
3. Dance, music, and theater camps
4. Day camps and school-hosted camps
5. History, nature, and science camps
6. Sports camps
7. STEM camps

That category spread is the minimum competitive bar for Hooky’s program map.

Mommy Poppins reinforces a narrower but important truth: competitors also package discovery around specific parent jobs, not just generic camp directories. The STEM-camp roundup functions as an intent-led collection.

Kids Out and About and ActivityHero reinforce the long tail:

- local enrichment businesses
- school-hosted camps
- multi-location chains
- niche sports and movement providers
- specialty STEM and maker providers

The implication is straightforward:

Hooky does **not** need to mimic their editorial packaging, but it **does** need to map the same provider universe or it will feel thin by comparison.

## Strategic Interpretation

### 1. Hooky’s moat should still start with structure, not breadth-for-breadth’s-sake

The competitor map is useful because it reveals the universe parents expect. But it does **not** mean Hooky should crawl every provider in the same order competitors list them.

Hooky should still prefer:

- structured providers
- official sources
- compareable metadata
- high-trust recurring supply

over:

- broad but low-signal provider sprawl

### 2. Competitive depth parity requires school and long-tail provider discovery

This is the important correction.

The first provider audit showed Hooky’s best near-term moat is museums, arts, coding, and swim. That remains true.

But competitor depth shows that **content completeness** in this market also requires:

- school-hosted camps
- long-tail STEM operators
- local sports/movement providers

Without those categories, Hooky risks feeling premium but incomplete.

### 3. The right model is “curator discovery, official-source execution”

This is the exact pattern used in LostCity’s curator strategy:

- competitors reveal who matters
- Hooky validates which providers are worth building
- official sites become the source of truth

This protects quality while still letting the market tell us where the important providers are.

## Bottom Line

Competitors are strongest on breadth, not structure.

Hooky is already strongest in premium structured family programming:

- museums
- arts
- coding
- swim

To reach real market depth parity, Hooky now needs to widen the map in a disciplined way:

- school-hosted camps
- long-tail STEM
- sports and movement
- local arts/music/dance providers

The correct move is not “crawl what competitors have.”

It is:

1. use competitors to reveal the provider universe
2. audit official provider sources for structure and trust
3. build only the providers that strengthen Hooky’s program moat and content completeness
